How it works

An assistant during the scan, not a replacement for the clinician.

SonoAI runs alongside the sonographer's existing workflow. It does not make diagnoses or replace clinical judgment — it watches the scan, measures, and flags, so the person in the room can act faster and with more confidence.

1

Scan, as usual

The clinician performs the ultrasound exactly as they normally would, using their existing handheld probe or scanner. No new hardware required.

2

SonoAI reads in real time

Trained computer vision models measure head circumference, biparietal diameter, and occipitofrontal diameter directly from the live image, and continuously check for signs of emergency findings.

3

Clinician reviews and acts

Measurements and flags are presented instantly on-screen. The clinician confirms findings and decides next steps — SonoAI surfaces the information, the human makes the call.

Why this matters

Most rural clinics in Kenya have an ultrasound machine. Almost none have a radiologist.

Roughly 70% of Kenyan healthcare providers have access to some form of ultrasound equipment, but a much smaller share use it regularly in practice — largely because there is no one on site qualified to interpret the scan. In many rural clinics, a woman is several times more likely to deliver without ever having had a scan read by a specialist.

SonoAI is built to close that interpretation gap directly at the point of care, using models trained specifically on the measurements that matter most for maternal and fetal safety.
